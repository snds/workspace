## Compositional Frameworks

Compositional frameworks are not rules — they are thinking tools that direct
attention and create visual intention. A designer who can only follow rules
produces mechanical compositions. A designer who understands why the frameworks
work can use, modify, or deliberately break them.

### Rule of Thirds

Divide the frame into a 3×3 grid (two vertical, two horizontal lines).
The four intersection points are the compositional "power points" — optically
active positions where the eye is naturally drawn.

**Application:**
- Place the primary subject at or near one of the four intersection points
- Place the horizon line at one of the two horizontal thirds (upper third for
  sky-dominated compositions; lower third for landscape/ground-dominated)
- Allow the subject to face or move into empty space (not into the frame edge)

**Why it works:** the eye is not drawn to the mathematical center — it naturally
gravitates to off-center positions that create visual tension. The rule of thirds
is an approximation of where that tension is productive.

### Golden Ratio (φ ≈ 1.618) Placement

The "phi grid" overlays the rule of thirds with golden ratio proportions —
the division lines are not at 1/3 but at 1/φ (≈ 0.382) from each edge.
The result is a tighter cluster of the compositional focal zones toward the
center of the frame.

**The golden spiral**: a spiral that follows the golden ratio's nested
rectangles — each new rectangle is 1/φ the size of the previous. Placing
the primary subject at the center of the spiral and leading the eye along
it produces the most harmonically resolved compositions.

**Practical application:** the rule of thirds is the working approximation.
The golden ratio is the refinement pass for compositions that need to feel
truly resolved. In fast-paced editorial work, the rule of thirds; in
carefully crafted campaign work, the golden spiral.

### Dynamic Symmetry

Jay Hambidge's early 20th-century system of "root rectangle" grids as
compositional organizing frameworks. A root-2 rectangle (√2 ≈ 1.414) contains
specific diagonal and harmonic intersection points that create natural
compositional anchors.

**Practical use:** complex editorial layouts with multiple elements; compositional
planning for large-format prints or murals where the rule of thirds feels too
simple.

### The Gestalt Figure/Ground Structure in Composition

Every photographic composition has a figure (the subject, what the eye attends
to) and a ground (the background, what the eye uses as context). The figure
must be legible against the ground — either through:
- **Value contrast**: subject lighter than background, or darker
- **Color contrast**: subject separated from background by hue or saturation difference
- **Focus differential**: subject in focus, background blurred (shallow depth of field)
- **Isolation**: space between the subject and background elements

When figure and ground compete (similar value, similar color, equal sharpness),
the composition loses its subject.

## Visual Weight

Visual weight determines where the eye goes and how long it stays. In a
composed frame, all elements have weight and must balance for the composition
to resolve.

**Factors that increase visual weight:**
- **Size**: larger elements are heavier
- **Value contrast**: a dark element on a light field weighs more than a
  light element on a light field; a light element on a dark field weighs more
  than a dark element on a dark field (because contrast, not absolute value,
  drives weight)
- **Saturation**: more saturated elements weigh more
- **Texture and complexity**: a textured or complex element weighs more than
  a smooth or simple one of the same area
- **Isolation**: an element surrounded by negative space weighs more than one
  in a group (because the space amplifies it)
- **Position**: elements near the top of the frame feel heavier than elements
  near the bottom (gravity is a perceptual metaphor); elements on the right
  of the frame feel heavier in LTR reading contexts (the eye lingers there)

**Compositional balance**: the distribution of visual weight across the frame.
Symmetric balance (equal weight, mirrored) creates formality and stability.
Asymmetric balance (different weight, counterbalanced) creates energy and
dynamism while still resolving to equilibrium.

## Negative Space

Negative space is the void — the areas of the image with no primary subject.
It is not empty; it is active.

**What negative space does:**
- **Breathing room**: creates separation between elements; the eye needs
  resting space before it can engage with the next element
- **Compositional weight**: a subject surrounded by space has more weight
  and presence than one crowded by competing elements
- **Directionality**: negative space can imply direction (a subject facing
  into open space suggests motion, future, possibility; a subject facing
  into a cluttered space feels trapped or pressured)
- **Tension**: space that is nearly empty but has subtle presence (a distant
  horizon, a shadow edge) creates compositional tension that holds attention

**Graphic design application:** negative space in a photographic image must
be compatible with the layout it will inhabit. An image with negative space
on the left allows text to be placed over that space without competing with
the subject. An image with wall-to-wall subject leaves no room for overlay.
**Art direct toward the layout**, not just toward the image as a standalone object.

## Lighting in Photography

Lighting is not a technical afterthought — it is a design decision that
determines mood, form, and legibility.

### Lighting Direction

**Front lighting** (source directly behind the camera):
- Flattens form; reduces shadow; increases apparent sharpness
- Appropriate for product shots where form documentation is primary;
  catalog photography; mug shots
- Creates even, unmodulated surfaces — can feel flat or clinical

**Side lighting (45°–90°)**:
- Reveals texture and three-dimensional form through shadow
- The most common and most versatile portrait and product lighting
- At 45°: reveals form without harsh shadow
- At 90°: maximum texture and shadow; dramatic; appropriate for character,
  craft, and material photography

**Back lighting / rim lighting**:
- Creates a luminous edge around the subject; separates subject from background
- Dramatic and cinematic; often combined with fill lighting to prevent
  silhouetting
- Appropriate for aspirational brand imagery, athletic photography,
  lifestyle contexts

**Top lighting / underlighting**:
- Top: skull-shadow effect on faces; used in horror/thriller contexts;
  generally unflattering for portraiture; works for some product photography
- Bottom: unnatural and unsettling; used deliberately for effect

### Lighting Quality

**Hard light** (small, concentrated source — direct sun, bare strobe):
- Defined, sharp-edged shadows
- High contrast between lit and shadow areas
- Dramatic, sculptural, assertive mood
- Reveals imperfection and texture (surfaces must be perfect)

**Soft light** (large, diffused source — overcast sky, softbox, bounce):
- Gradual, feathered shadows
- Lower contrast
- Flattering, approachable, natural mood
- Conceals texture; more forgiving of surface imperfections

**The practical rule:** match light quality to the brand and subject. Hard
light for editorial, fashion, architecture, craft (texture and form matter).
Soft light for food, beauty, lifestyle, portraiture (approachability and
comfort matter).

### Color Temperature

**Warm light** (candles, tungsten, golden hour sun): approximately 2700–3500K.
Produces yellow/amber cast. Associations: intimacy, warmth, comfort, history,
craft, domesticity.

**Neutral light** (midday sun, studio flash): approximately 5000–6500K.
Associations: documentary, product, technical, professional.

**Cool light** (blue sky, shade, overcast): approximately 7000–10000K.
Associations: calm, clinical, cold, contemporary, technological.

**Design application:** color temperature in photography must harmonize with
the brand palette. A warm-toned brand (orange, amber, cream) requires
photography in warm light. A cool-toned brand (blue, white, silver) requires
cool-neutral photography. Mixed color temperatures within a single brand
campaign create incoherence.

## Image-Type Relationships

When typography and photography coexist in a layout, they must be designed
as a system — not as independent elements that happen to share space.

### Scale Relationships

The typographic scale must be designed in relationship to the image:
- Large typography over a full-bleed image must be legible against the
  image's tonal range (test with the darkest and lightest versions of
  the image in the campaign)
- Small typography near an image must not compete visually with the
  image's texture and detail

### Contrast Management

Typography placed over photography requires a consistent contrast strategy:

**Text areas within the image**: art direct image captures to include areas
of low visual complexity (open sky, plain wall, solid surface) where text
will be placed.

**Design solutions for contrast:**
- Gradient overlay: transparent-to-solid color gradient behind text area
- Color wash: semi-transparent colored panel behind text
- Text shadow / drop shadow: functional but signals lower production value
  if overused
- Frosted panel: blurred and lightened/darkened copy of the image behind text

**Typography should never compete with the primary subject of the image.**
If text must appear over a complex area of the image, the text becomes the
problem to solve (color, weight, size) — not the image.

### Image and Text Register

Images and text carry different emotional registers; they must be compatible:

- A clinical, high-contrast photograph paired with warm, handwritten typography
  creates a register conflict
- A playful, saturated illustration paired with austere serif typography
  creates a register conflict
- Register alignment: photography style, illustration style, typeface choice,
  and copy tone must all occupy the same emotional territory

## Illustration Styles

When directing illustration, the style choice is a brand and communication
decision as much as an aesthetic one.

### Line Illustration

Contour drawings; the subject is defined by line, not fill.

- **Strengths**: clean, direct, scalable; works at any size; reproduces well
  in one or two colors
- **Associations**: classic editorial, technical documentation, fine art
  reference (Old Masters drawings), modern minimal editorial
- **Use when**: the brand is sophisticated and restrained; when the image must
  work at multiple scales; when color printing is limited

### Flat Illustration

Shapes defined by color fills, no gradients, minimal or no shadow.

- **Strengths**: highly adaptable to motion and screen; modern and flexible;
  integrates with UI
- **Associations**: technology brands, startup culture, education, contemporary
  editorial
- **Use when**: the brand is modern and approachable; when illustrations will
  also be animated; when they must integrate with UI contexts

### Textured Illustration

Flat illustration enhanced with grain, noise, or halftone textures — analog
media quality in a digital execution.

- **Strengths**: warmth and distinctiveness; analog feel without literal
  photography
- **Associations**: craft, food, sustainability, independent brands, nostalgia
- **Use when**: the brand values authenticity, warmth, or handmade quality

### Painterly Illustration

Oil, watercolor, gouache, or digital equivalents — marks visible, medium
present.

- **Strengths**: high emotional register; unmistakably handmade; distinctive
- **Associations**: luxury, narrative, art, editorial depth, children's
  publishing, heritage brands
- **Use when**: the image must have emotional depth and uniqueness over reproducibility

## Art Direction: Briefing Photographers and Illustrators

The quality of a creative brief determines the quality of what is produced.
"We need some photos of people using the product" is not a brief.

### Photography Brief Components

**Concept**: what is the creative idea? What should the image communicate?
Not "show the product" but "show the ease and confidence of someone who
has this product in their life."

**Mood board**: 8–15 reference images that establish aesthetic direction.
References for: lighting quality and direction, color temperature, subject
treatment, styling, environmental context.

**Shot list**: explicit list of every required image, with:
- Subject/scene
- Orientation (portrait/landscape/square)
- Primary use (full-bleed hero / small feature / social square)
- Notes on subject treatment and composition

**Brand guardrails**: typeface overlay areas, negative space requirements,
color palette context, prohibited visual elements.

**Technical specs**: final delivery format (RAW + processed TIFF; resolution;
color space sRGB vs. Adobe RGB), any special requirements.

### Illustration Brief Components

**Style reference**: examples that define the specific style — character
weight, color palette, level of detail, edge treatment.

**Scale of use**: the smallest and largest sizes at which the illustration
will appear (determines minimum detail level).

**Color palette**: brand colors to use; whether illustration should introduce
new colors or stay within the established palette.

**Subject and symbolic requirements**: what the illustration must depict,
and whether specific symbols must be included or avoided.

**File delivery**: vector (AI/EPS/SVG) vs. raster; resolution; layering
requirements.

## Image Selection Criteria

Selecting images from a shoot or stock library is an editorial act. Criteria:

**Technical quality**: resolution adequate for intended use; focus sharp on
the subject; exposure correct; no compression artifacts.

**Emotional register**: does the image feel consistent with the brand emotional
territory? Not just "positive" but specifically what kind of positive — confident,
warm, energetic, calm?

**Composition**: does the image work in the layout? Does it have the required
negative space? Does the subject placement support the typographic overlay?

**Cultural representation**: who is depicted? Does the image accurately represent
the brand's actual and intended audience? Images that depict a narrower or
less representative slice of humanity than the actual audience are a trust signal
failure — they tell certain users "this is not for you."

**Brand alignment**: does the image's visual vocabulary (color, light, styling,
environment) align with the brand's established visual language?

## Anti-Patterns

- **Center-heavy composition by default.** Placing the subject dead center
  produces static, non-designed compositions. The rule of thirds provides
  a minimal starting point.
- **No space for typography.** Selecting images without considering where
  text will live. Art direct toward the layout.
- **Mismatched register.** Illustrations that feel playful against a brand
  that wants to feel authoritative, or vice versa.
- **Generic stock photography.** Images that depict a world where everyone
  is happy, symmetrical, and in business casual. The artificiality signals
  low investment in brand craft.
- **Color temperature drift within a campaign.** Multiple images with
  different white balance creates visual incoherence when seen together.
- **Dark images without contrast strategy.** Type placed over complex,
  dark images without a contrast solution is illegible.
- **Illustration brief without a style reference.** "We need illustrations
  that are friendly and modern" produces whatever the illustrator considers
  friendly and modern, which is rarely what was intended.
