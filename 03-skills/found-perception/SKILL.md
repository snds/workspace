# Foundations — Perception

How the eye parses an image before the mind reads it. These laws are physiological, so they hold in any
medium. Design works *with* them or fights them.

## Gestalt grouping
The eye groups before it interprets: **proximity** (near = related — the strongest grouping cue),
**similarity** (alike = related), **continuity** (the eye follows lines/curves), **closure** (it completes
implied shapes), **common region** (shared container = group), **common fate** (things moving together =
group). Most "this feels cluttered" problems are proximity/region failures, not decoration failures.

## Figure / ground
The eye must separate subject from background. Ambiguous figure/ground (insufficient contrast, competing
emphasis) is read as effort. Establish one clear figure; let everything else recede.

## Pre-attentive processing
A few channels are read in <250ms, before conscious attention: **color hue, intensity, orientation, size,
motion, position**. These are the only channels that "pop out" instantly — which is why hierarchy and data
encoding lean on them. Stack more than ~2 and the pop-out collapses.

## Visual weight + optical adjustment
Elements have perceived weight (size, value, color, density, isolation) independent of their measured
dimensions. **The eye outranks the math:** circles must overshoot the baseline to look the same size as
squares; centered things often need optical, not geometric, centering; equal spacing rarely *looks* equal.
Trust the eye as the final validator.
