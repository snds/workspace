# Foundations — Hierarchy

Hierarchy is the message before the message: what the eye should see first, second, third. Every medium
needs it; the encodings are universal.

## Contrast is the engine
Nothing reads as important without something to be more important *than*. Hierarchy is **differences**, not
absolutes — a "big" heading is only big relative to the body. Insufficient contrast between levels is the
single most common hierarchy failure: three sizes that are nearly the same read as one flat level.

## The encoding channels
Priority is signalled by, roughly in order of force: **size**, **weight**, **color/value** (a saturated or
high-contrast element jumps), **position** (top-left in LTR reading; optical center), **space** (isolation
confers importance — a thing alone reads as primary), **case/style**. These map onto the pre-attentive
channels in [[found-perception]]. **Use the fewest channels that achieve the separation** — stacking all of
them on one element is shouting.

## Levels, not gradients
Design *discrete* levels (primary / secondary / tertiary), each clearly distinct. A continuum of slightly
different treatments gives the eye no anchors. Three to four levels handle almost everything; more than that,
and you've usually got a structure problem, not a styling problem.

## Restraint
The strongest emphasis is created by restraint elsewhere. If everything is bold, nothing is. Make one thing
primary and *let the rest recede* — most "make this pop" requests are solved by quieting the surroundings.
