## Type Anatomy Vocabulary

Before classification, the vocabulary for describing letterforms:

### Structural Elements

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| Serif | Small perpendicular stroke at the end of a main stroke |
| Counter | Enclosed or partially enclosed negative space within a letterform (the hole in 'o', the space in 'c') |
| Bowl | The curved closed or nearly closed part of a letter (in 'b', 'd', 'p', 'q', 'g') |
| Stem | The primary vertical stroke |
| Crossbar | Horizontal connecting stroke (in 'A', 'H', 'e', 'f', 't') |
| Spine | The main curved stroke of 'S' and 's' |
| Arm | A horizontal or diagonal stroke that is free at one or both ends (in 'E', 'F', 'K', 'Y') |
| Leg | A downward diagonal stroke that is free at the bottom (in 'K', 'R') |
| Shoulder | The curved part of 'n', 'm', 'h' where the arched stroke begins |
| Tail | A descending stroke at the end of a letter ('Q', 'R', 'j', 'y') |
| Ear | A small projection from the top of the lowercase 'g' or 'r' |
| Link | The connecting stroke between the top and bottom counters of a two-story 'g' |
| Aperture | The opening of partially open counters ('c', 'e', 'a', 's') |
| Finial | A curved, tapered, or ball-shaped terminal |

### Optical Properties

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| Stress axis | The angle of the transition between thick and thin strokes; vertical stress = Didone tradition; diagonal stress = humanist/calligraphic tradition |
| x-height | Height of lowercase letters (specifically 'x') relative to capitals |
| Contrast | Ratio between thick strokes and thin strokes |
| Bracket | The curved transition between a serif and the main stroke; "bracketed serif" vs. "unbracketed serif" |

## Vox-ATypI Classification

The standard classification system for Latin typefaces, developed by Maximilien
Vox in 1954 and adopted by ATypI in 1962.

### Classicals (Old Style)

**Humanist (Venetian)**
- Origin: Venice, 1470s — earliest Roman type
- Characteristics: diagonal stress (calligraphic), slanted crossbar on lowercase 'e', bracketed serifs, low contrast, open forms
- Examples: Jenson, Centaur, Cloister
- Register: archaic, literary, humanist

**Garalde (Old Style)**
- Origin: France, 16th century — Garamond era
- Characteristics: diagonal stress, but more refined than humanist; higher contrast; horizontal crossbar on lowercase 'e' (distinguishing from humanist); graceful proportions
- Examples: Garamond, Caslon, Palatino, Minion, Adobe Jenson (modern revival), Sabon
- Register: literary, classical, warm, authoritative without being cold

**Transitional**
- Origin: 18th century England/France
- Characteristics: vertical or near-vertical stress axis; higher contrast; sharper serifs; more geometric regularity; between calligraphic tradition and neoclassical
- Examples: Baskerville, Times New Roman, Perpetua, Caslon (later cuts), Georgia
- Register: formal, authoritative, neutral, institutional; Times is the default of institutional authority

### Moderns (Neoclassical)

**Didone**
- Origin: Late 18th century France and Italy (Didot, Bodoni)
- Characteristics: extreme vertical stress; extreme contrast (very thin hairlines, very thick stems); horizontal unbracketed serifs (no bracket); mathematical precision
- Examples: Bodoni, Didot, Walbaum, Linotype Didot
- Register: elegance, luxury, fashion, precision, formality; the typeface of fashion magazines

### Slab Serifs

**Mechanistic (Slab)**
- Origin: 19th century — industrial era advertising
- Characteristics: heavy, unbracketed slab serifs; low contrast or monolinear; mechanical, geometric quality
- Examples: Rockwell, Memphis, Clarendon, Archer, Sentinel
- Register: industrial, assertive, American; used in advertising, editorial, western contexts

### Sans-Serifs

**Grotesque (Lineal — Grotesque)**
- Origin: 19th century — first commercial sans-serifs
- Characteristics: low contrast; irregular; closed apertures; no calligraphic influence; practical rather than refined
- Examples: Akzidenz Grotesk, Franklin Gothic, Trade Gothic, News Gothic
- Register: workhorse, practical, pre-modernist neutrality

**Neo-grotesque (Lineal — Neo-grotesque)**
- Origin: Mid-20th century — Swiss/German modernism
- Characteristics: highly regularized grotesque; very closed apertures; near-perfect monolinear strokes; mechanical uniformity; deliberately neutral
- Examples: Helvetica, Univers, Folio, Arial
- Register: universal, neutral, institutional, corporate Switzerland; neutrality can feel cold

**Geometric (Lineal — Geometric)**
- Origin: Bauhaus, 1920s
- Characteristics: forms based on geometric shapes — circular 'o', minimal strokes, monolinear; highly rationalized
- Examples: Futura, Avenir, Gill Sans (transitional), Brandon Grotesque, Montserrat
- Register: modernist, clean, idealist, visionary, technological; Futura = future, optimism

**Humanist sans (Lineal — Humanist)**
- Origin: Mid-20th century, drawing on calligraphic tradition
- Characteristics: variable stroke width (slight contrast); calligraphic axis; open apertures; more organic than geometric
- Examples: Frutiger, Myriad, Gill Sans, Optima, Trebuchet, Calibri
- Register: legible, approachable, human-centered; Frutiger was designed for airport signage — peak functional legibility

### Scripts and Display

**Glyphic**: Letterforms with calligraphic or lapidary (carved stone) influence (Trajan, Copperplate)
**Script**: Designs that imitate handwriting (Zapf Chancery, casual script faces)
**Blackletter/Textura**: Medieval manuscript tradition (Fraktur, Rotunda)
**Monospace**: Fixed-advance-width type for code (Courier, Consolas, JetBrains Mono)

## Key Typefaces and Cultural Context

Understanding the cultural register of a typeface is understanding how and when
to deploy it. Choosing incorrectly creates category errors that undermine the
communication.

| Typeface | Classification | Cultural register | Use; avoid when |
|---|---|---|---|
| Garamond | Garalde | French Renaissance humanism, literary elegance, scholarly authority | Literary publishing, editorial, academic; avoid for fintech, tech startups |
| Caslon | Garalde | English pragmatism, American tradition (Declaration of Independence was set in Caslon) | American heritage brands, newspapers, editorial; avoid for international/European registers |
| Baskerville | Transitional | Enlightenment clarity, rationalism, authority | Academic, legal, editorial authority; avoid for warmth, friendliness |
| Bodoni | Didone | Neoclassical elegance, luxury, precision, fashion | Fashion, luxury goods, high-end editorial; avoid for body text, approachable contexts |
| Garamond Italic | Garalde | Emphasis, humanist annotation | Running text for titles, sidebars; avoid using alone as primary face |
| Gill Sans | Humanist sans | British modernism, BBC, London Underground — public, civic, approachable modernity | British brands, public services, humanist tech; avoid when needing neutrality (it carries UK connotations) |
| Helvetica | Neo-grotesque | Swiss neutrality, international modernism, universal — "design by refusing to decide" | Corporate identity, signage, when neutrality is the message; avoid when warmth or character is needed |
| Univers | Neo-grotesque | Systematic design (Frutiger's grid system), rational variation | Design systems requiring systematic weight/width variants; similar register to Helvetica but more systematic |
| Futura | Geometric | Bauhaus idealism, futurism, modernity, technological optimism | Tech, modernist brands, forward-looking identities; avoid for warm, traditional, or historical contexts |
| Frutiger | Humanist sans | Legibility as design goal — designed for Charles de Gaulle Airport signage | Wayfinding, healthcare, public information, anything requiring high legibility |
| FF Meta | Humanist sans | Postmodern complexity, information design, Erik Spiekermann's pragmatism | Information-dense design, editorial, design industry; avoid for luxury or historical contexts |
| Rockwell | Mechanistic slab | Industrial, American, bold statement | Display, advertising, editorial punch; avoid for extended reading |
| Trajan | Glyphic (all caps) | Ancient Rome, authority, epic — used in every historical epic film poster | Film, dramatic institutional contexts; avoid for body text (no lowercase), casual or approachable contexts |

## Type Pairing Logic

Good typeface pairing follows two simultaneous principles:

### Contrast
Different pairs that distinguish primary from secondary type:
- Different classification (serif body + sans-serif heading, or vice versa)
- Different weight (bold heading + regular body)
- Different proportion (condensed vs. normal, high vs. low x-height)

### Harmony
Shared qualities that make the pair feel coordinated:
- Shared historical period (Garamond body + Trajan headline — both classical)
- Complementary stress axes (a humanist serif with a humanist sans)
- Compatible x-heights (pairing fonts of wildly different x-heights creates optical chaos)
- Intentional contrast (not accidental similarity — two different neo-grotesques fighting for identity)

### Classic Pairings

| Heading | Body | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Any sans | Garamond, Caslon, or Minion | The quintessential editorial combination |
| Bodoni | Futura | Neoclassical precision + modernist geometry — sophisticated contrast |
| Helvetica | Any slab serif | Swiss neutrality + mechanical warmth |
| A serif display | Frutiger or Myriad | Authority + readability |
| Same family, different weights | Humanist variation | Clean, systematic, safe |

**Anti-pattern**: Two similar-but-different typefaces in the same classification
competing for identity (two neo-grotesques, two garaldes). They don't pair — they
conflict. The result looks like a mistake, not a decision.

## Typeface Selection Criteria

When choosing a typeface for a project, evaluate against these criteria:

1. **Legibility at intended sizes**: Does it read well at the smallest size it will appear?
2. **Character disambiguation**: Can 0/O, l/1/I, rn/m be distinguished? Critical for data, UI, code.
3. **Language support**: Does it include all characters needed for the project's languages?
4. **Licensing**: Is the license appropriate for the deployment (web, app, broadcast, product)?
5. **Web/variable font availability**: Is a quality WOFF2 available? Is there a variable font?
6. **Weight and style coverage**: Does it have the weights and styles needed (regular, bold, italic minimum)?
7. **Design system compatibility**: Does it integrate with existing DS type tokens? Does its x-height align reasonably with companion fonts?
8. **Historical register alignment**: Does the typeface's cultural context match the project's communication register?

## Anti-Patterns and Failure Modes

| Anti-pattern | Symptom | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Trajan for a tech startup | Looks like a movie poster for a historical epic | Choose a humanist or geometric sans |
| Comic Sans anywhere professional | Immediately destroys credibility | Choose based on register, not familiarity |
| Two neo-grotesques paired | Looks like an inconsistency, not a decision | Choose one; or switch classification for the pair |
| Arbitrary multiplier type scale | Steps don't reflect optical size perception | Base scale on actual perceptual differences; use optical sizing knowledge |
| Font chosen for aesthetic only | Fails at its operational size, wrong cultural register | Test at deployment size and evaluate register |
| Missing language support discovered in QA | UI broken in production for non-Latin locales | Check language coverage before finalizing |
